# Deep Cleaning Records

While a carbon-fiber brush is great for knocking off surface dust before you drop the needle, it does not actually *clean* the record. To remove the static charge, fingerprints, mold-release compounds from the pressing plant, and deep-seated grime, you need to wet-clean the record.

## Why Wet Clean?

- **Drastically reduces surface noise:** Pops, clicks, and crackles are often dirt, not scratches.
- **Protects your stylus:** Dragging a diamond needle through dirt at high speeds grinds down the diamond much faster.
- **Removes static:** Static electricity acts like a magnet for dust and causes loud "pops" during playback. Wet cleaning eliminates the static charge.

## Level 1: The Manual Bath (Budget: $60-$80)

The most popular budget option is the **Spin-Clean Record Washer System**.

**How it works:**
You fill a narrow basin with distilled water and a capful of specialized cleaning fluid. Two velvet brushes sit inside the basin. You slot the record in, manually spin it three times clockwise and three times counterclockwise, and then dry it with the supplied microfiber cloths.

- **Pros:** Inexpensive, highly effective at removing deep dirt, no moving parts to break.
- **Cons:** Manual labor. Drying the records with cloths can sometimes introduce static if you rub too hard. You have to change the bath water after 20-30 records.

## Level 2: Vacuum Record Cleaning Machines (Budget: $200-$500)

Vacuum RCMs, like the **Record Doctor V** or the **Pro-Ject VC-E**, are the standard for serious collectors.

**How it works:**
You place the record on a platter, apply cleaning fluid, and scrub it into the grooves with a brush. Then, you turn on a vacuum motor that sucks all the dirty fluid directly out of the grooves, leaving the record perfectly dry in seconds.

- **Pros:** Sucking the dirt out is far superior to wiping it off. Records are perfectly dry and immediately ready to play.
- **Cons:** The vacuum motors are incredibly loud (like a shop vac).

## Level 3: Ultrasonic Cleaners (Budget: $400-$2000+)

Ultrasonic machines use cavitation (microscopic bubbles created by high-frequency sound waves) to blast dirt out of the deepest parts of the groove without anything physically touching the record.

**How it works:**
Records are slowly rotated through a bath of distilled water while ultrasonic transducers bombard them. Examples include the HumminGuru (affordable end) or the Degritter (high end).

- **Pros:** The absolute most thorough clean possible. Entirely hands-off.
- **Cons:** Very expensive, and sometimes slower per-record than a vacuum machine.

## Best Practices for Any Method

1. **Only use Distilled Water:** Never use tap water. Tap water contains minerals that will dry inside the grooves and ruin your records.
2. **Avoid Isopropyl Alcohol:** Some DIY recipes call for heavy alcohol use. While a tiny amount can act as a surfactant, too much alcohol will leach the plasticizers out of the vinyl, making it brittle over time. Stick to trusted commercial fluids like Tergitol-based solutions.
3. **Change the Inner Sleeve:** Never put a freshly cleaned record back into a dirty paper sleeve. Always use a new anti-static poly-lined sleeve.
