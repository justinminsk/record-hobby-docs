# Headphones & Vinyl

Listening to records through headphones is an incredibly immersive experience. It allows you to pick out tiny details in the mastering that you would never hear through speakers in a typical room. However, there is a technical hurdle that beginners often miss: **you cannot just plug headphones into a turntable.**

## 1. How to Connect Headphones

Turntables output a very weak signal. To hear music through headphones, that signal needs to be amplified *twice*.

1. **Phono Preamp:** Boosts the tiny signal to standard "Line Level" and applies the RIAA equalization curve (adding back the bass that was stripped out during pressing).
2. **Headphone Amplifier:** Takes that Line Level signal and provides the electrical current necessary to physically move the heavy drivers inside your headphones.

### The Signal Chain
Your setup must look like this: 
`Turntable` ➔ `Phono Preamp` ➔ `Headphone Amp` ➔ `Headphones`

### How to achieve this:
- **The Vintage Route:** If you have an old stereo receiver (like an old Pioneer or Sony from the 70s/80s), it likely has both a Phono Preamp and a Headphone Amp built directly into it. Just plug your turntable into the "Phono" input and your headphones into the front jack.
- **The Modern Route:** If you have a modern turntable with a built-in phono preamp (like a Fluance RT81 or LP120X), you only need to buy a standalone **Headphone Amplifier**. 
  - *Budget Kings:* The **[Schiit Magni](https://www.schiit.com/products/magni-unity)** or the **[JDS Labs Atom Amp](https://jdslabs.com/product/atom-amp/)**. Connect your turntable's RCA cables into the back of the headphone amp, and plug your headphones into the front.

## 2. Open-Back vs. Closed-Back

When shopping for high-end headphones, you will see them categorized by the design of the ear cup.

### Open-Back Headphones (The Gold Standard)
The back of the ear cup is completely open (often covered by a metal grill).
- **Pros:** They sound massive. The music sounds like it is playing in the room around you (known as a wide "soundstage"), rather than trapped inside your head.
- **Cons:** They leak sound out, and they let all outside noise in. You cannot use them in public or in a loud room.

### Closed-Back Headphones
The back of the ear cup is sealed with solid plastic or wood.
- **Pros:** Excellent isolation. They block out your roommates and have much punchier sub-bass impact.
- **Cons:** The sound is "in your head," and your ears can get hot over long listening sessions.

## 3. Excellent Starter Headphones

If you want to experience true high-fidelity audio without spending thousands of dollars, these are the universal benchmarks:

- **[Sennheiser HD6XX](https://drop.com/buy/massdrop-sennheiser-hd6xx) (or HD650) - Open-Back:** 
  The legendary king of the mid-range. Voices, guitars, and jazz sound incredibly natural and lush. They require a dedicated headphone amplifier to sound their best because they have high impedance (300 ohms).
- **[Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro](https://north-america.beyerdynamic.com/dt-770-pro.html) - Closed-Back:** 
  A durable, classic closed-back option. They have excellent bass extension and sparkling treble, making them great for electronic, rock, and hip-hop.
- **[Audio-Technica ATH-M50x](https://www.audio-technica.com/en-us/ath-m50x) - Closed-Back:** 
  The classic studio monitor. They fold up easily and provide a very analytical, punchy sound. They are highly efficient, meaning you can plug them directly into your phone or a very weak amp and they will still get loud.
