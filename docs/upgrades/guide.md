# Upgrades Guide

Upgrading a vinyl setup can be a bottomless pit. To avoid wasting money, it helps to understand which upgrades actually make an audible difference and the logical order in which to approach them.

## The Upgrade Hierarchy

When you are ready to spend money to improve your sound, this is generally the most effective order:

1. **Speakers:** The single biggest difference you can make.
2. **Speaker Placement & Room Acoustics:** Free (or cheap) and drastically changes how your speakers perform.
3. **Stylus/Cartridge:** Directly reads the groove; a better profile means more detail and less distortion.
4. **Phono Preamp:** Can lower the noise floor and provide better dynamics, but only if the rest of your system is good enough to reveal the difference.
5. **Turntable (Deck/Tonearm):** A more stable deck reduces vibration and speed variations.
6. **Amplifier:** Important, but modern amps are generally very capable; upgrade this when moving to demanding passive speakers.

## 1. Upgrading Speakers

If you started with powered speakers (Path A) and want a massive leap in sound quality, the path forward is usually transitioning to **passive speakers and a dedicated stereo amplifier (Path C)**.

- **Why:** Passive speakers offer a much wider range of choices, better performance-to-price ratios in the mid-to-high tier, and you don't have to throw out the amp if a speaker blows.
- **Deep Dive:** If you want to understand exactly why speakers are the most critical component of the chain, watch [Audioholics' breakdowns on speaker design and physics](https://www.youtube.com/user/Audioholics).

## 2. Upgrading the Stylus or Cartridge

This is the most common and rewarding turntable upgrade. Upgrading your stylus profile (the shape of the diamond) is the best way to eliminate Inner-Groove Distortion (IGD).

- **Elliptical vs. Microlinear:** Most beginner turntables come with an elliptical stylus. Upgrading to a Microlinear (ML) or Shibata stylus allows the needle to sit deeper in the groove, tracking higher frequencies flawlessly.
- **Stylus Upgrade:** If you have a popular cartridge (like the Ortofon 2M Red or Audio-Technica AT-VM95E), you can drastically improve the sound simply by sliding off the stylus and sliding on a better one from the same family (e.g., Ortofon 2M Blue, or AT-VM95ML).
- **Cartridge Upgrade:** Changing to a completely different cartridge requires you to carefully align it using a protractor and reset the tracking force. [Turntable Lab provides an excellent visual guide for this](../explore/community-resources.md).

## 3. External Phono Preamp

If you are using the built-in phono preamp in your turntable or powered speakers, an external phono preamp can be a great upgrade—*if* your speakers are revealing enough.

- **Benefits:** Lower background noise, better instrument separation, and punchier dynamics.
- **Reviews:** Before buying an external preamp, check [What Hi-Fi?](https://www.whathifi.com/) or [The Audiophiliac](https://www.youtube.com/c/SteveGuttenbergAudiophiliac) for reviews on budget kings like the Schiit Mani or iFi Zen Phono.
- **Note:** Make sure to turn *off* the turntable's built-in preamp (usually a switch marked "Phono/Line" - set it to "Phono") when using an external one, or you will double-amplify the signal and get terrible distortion.

## What to Avoid Early On

Don't spend money on these until you have a high-end system:
- **Expensive cables:** Standard, well-shielded cables are perfectly fine. Do not fall for $200 RCA cable snake oil.
- **Record weights/clamps:** Rarely make a noticeable difference on entry-level turntables and can add wear to the motor if too heavy.
- **Acrylic/Cork/Leather mats:** Fun for aesthetics and minor static reduction, but the sonic differences are incredibly subtle.
