# Troubleshooting Guide

When setting up a turntable for the first time, or after moving it, you might run into a few common issues. Before assuming your equipment is broken, check these frequent culprits.

## 1. Constant Low-Pitch Humming

A loud, constant "hum" or "buzz" that gets louder when you turn up the volume is usually a **ground loop**. Turntables generate very tiny signals that are highly susceptible to interference.

### How to fix it:
- **Check the Ground Wire:** If your turntable has a separate ground wire (usually a thin wire with a U-shaped spade connector at the end), ensure it is tightly screwed into the "GND" terminal on your amplifier or external phono preamp.
- **Move the Turntable:** Don't place your turntable directly on top of your receiver/amplifier, and keep it away from Wi-Fi routers or large power bricks.
- **Built-in Preamps:** If you are using a turntable with a built-in phono preamp and powered speakers, you often *do not* need to attach a ground wire.

## 2. Records Keep Skipping

If your needle constantly jumps out of the groove, especially during loud or bass-heavy sections, the problem is usually the setup, not the record.

### How to fix it:
- **Level the Deck:** Use a small spirit level to ensure the platter is perfectly flat. A tilted turntable will pull the tonearm.
- **Check Tracking Force:** Ensure your counterweight is set to the cartridge manufacturer's recommended tracking force (e.g., 2.0 grams). If it's too light, the needle will bounce out of the groove.
- **Suitcase Players:** If you are using a cheap all-in-one suitcase player, skipping on modern, heavily-pressed records is a known mechanical limitation of the cheap spring-loaded tonearm mechanism.

## 3. The Sound is Extremely Quiet and "Tinny"

If you turn the volume way up and the music sounds thin, scratchy, and has no bass, your signal is not passing through a **Phono Preamp**.

### How to fix it:
- **Turn the Preamp ON:** If your turntable has a switch on the back labeled "Phono/Line", set it to "Line" if you are plugging directly into powered speakers or a standard "Aux" input.
- **Use a Phono Input:** If you are using an external amplifier, ensure you are plugged into the input explicitly labeled "Phono". 

## 4. The Sound is Distorted, Loud, and Fuzzy

If the music sounds incredibly loud, distorted, and unlistenable at low volumes, you are double-amplifying the signal.

### How to fix it:
- **Turn the Preamp OFF:** If you have plugged your turntable into a receiver's "Phono" input, you must set the switch on the back of the turntable to "Phono". Leaving it on "Line" means the signal is amplified twice.

## 5. Distortion Near the Center of the Record

This is known as **Inner-Groove Distortion (IGD)**. As the needle gets closer to the center label, the physical grooves become much more compressed, making them harder to track accurately.

### How to fix it:
- **Clean your Stylus:** Dust buildup exacerbates tracking issues.
- **Check Alignment:** If you installed the cartridge yourself, double-check it with an alignment protractor.
- **Stylus Upgrade:** Upgrading from an elliptical stylus to a microlinear (ML) or Shibata stylus drastically reduces IGD because those shapes fit deeper and more precisely into the groove.

## 6. One Channel is Dead

If sound only comes out of the left or right speaker:

### How to fix it:
- **Check RCA Cables:** Ensure they are fully seated. Swap the red and white cables to see if the problem moves to the other speaker (if it does, the issue is before the amp).
- **Check Cartridge Leads:** Look at the four tiny colored wires connecting the cartridge to the tonearm (or headshell). Carefully ensure they are all pushed firmly onto their pins using tweezers. Do not pull on the wires themselves!
