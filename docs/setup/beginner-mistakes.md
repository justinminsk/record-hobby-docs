# Common Beginner Mistakes

When you are new to the vinyl hobby, there are a few "unwritten rules" that aren't printed in the turntable manual. To avoid damaging your records or your gear, avoid these common pitfalls.

## 1. Handling the Record Like a Frisbee

The single fastest way to ruin how a record sounds is by getting the oils from your skin into the grooves. Skin oils act like glue, trapping dust deep inside the groove where your cleaning brush can't reach it, causing permanent crackles.

- **The Rule:** Only ever touch the extreme outer edge of the record and the paper label in the center. When pulling it out of the sleeve, bow the sleeve slightly, slide your hand in to support the center label, and use your thumb on the edge to guide it out.

## 2. Stacking Records Flat

Records are extremely heavy. If you stack them flat on top of each other (like pancakes), two things will happen:
1. The immense weight will permanently warp the records on the bottom of the pile.
2. The pressure will press the vinyl ring into the cardboard jacket, permanently rubbing off the cover art in a circle (known as "ring wear").

- **The Rule:** Always store records completely vertically, like books on a shelf. 
- **The Setup Standard:** If you need a shelf, buy an **IKEA Kallax**. It is the undisputed, universal standard for record storage. The cubes are perfectly sized (13x13 inches) and the shelving is structurally strong enough to hold the immense weight of a vinyl collection.

## 3. Moving a Turntable Fully Assembled

If you are moving to a new apartment, or even just moving your turntable to a friend's house for the night, you cannot simply pick it up and put it in the back of a car. 

- **The Rule:** You *must* remove the heavy counterweight from the back of the tonearm. If you hit a bump while driving, that bouncing weight will snap the delicate bearings inside the tonearm, ruining the turntable.
- You should also take the heavy platter off and secure the tonearm to its rest with a twist-tie.

## 4. Playing with the Dust Cover Closed

This is a hotly debated topic, but strictly speaking, your turntable will sound better if the plastic dust cover is open or completely removed while playing.

- **The Reason:** A closed plastic box acts like an acoustic microphone. It catches the loud bass frequencies bouncing off the walls from your speakers and feeds those vibrations right back into the turntable base and the stylus. This creates a "muddy" sound. 
- Keep the cover closed when you aren't listening to keep the dust out, but lift it up when the needle drops!

## 5. Dropping the Needle by Hand

Unless you have incredibly steady hands, trying to manually drop the stylus directly onto the spinning record is a great way to accidentally drop it *next* to the record, destroying the stylus on the rubber mat.

- **The Rule:** Always use the cue lever (the small switch that raises and lowers the tonearm hydraulically). Position the arm over the record, and flip the lever to let it lower gently and perfectly straight.
